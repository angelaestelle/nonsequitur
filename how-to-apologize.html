how to apologize

"An apology may be one of the least costly and most rewarding investments you can make."
– Roger Fisher and William Ury (Getting to Yes)

Background Info

In November 2021, we created a series of greeting cards called Remorse Code that offered a simple, friendly step-by-step, fill-in-the-blank style communication device for your relational fuck-ups. The idea was that apologizing (and real apologizing, not the half-hearted obligatory kind) is a fundamental part of healthy relationships. Honing your apologizing skills adds to your communication toolkit, and allows for you to deepen all the relationship essentials: trust, connection, vulnerability, compassion, and empathy. Below we offer the cards as a free PDF for download, in addition to further information and tips on how to use them. You can also use the cards as a self reflection tool, processing a mishap, or as a jumping point as journal prompts to apologize to yourself. We hope that this tool eases the process a little, and helps you to do the difficult (but right) thing. At the end is further reading and more resources for apology processes. Good luck!

Disclaimer (read this and think)

Remorse Code is for low-level, not (too) serious, mistakes, slip ups, and faults that we all commit and experience: forgetting a friend's birthday, not doing your half of the household chores, a passive aggressive comment towards a coworker. We all mess up from time-to-time and hurt feelings unintentionally. Not addressing things can lead to bigger problems like misunderstanding and resentment. Apologizing is HARD but it starts with taking accountability for what happened. However, for more serious harms like cheating or manipulation, you will need to take the apology beyond this card. Use your discretion and relationship history to determine your personalized apology action plan. Building a culture of accountability begins with mastering the apology, as uncomfortable as it may be. Start with yourself. Start small.

Who this is for
  • people who had or are in an interpersonal conflict involving mistakes, regrets, and slip-ups new or old
  • people who have to continue be around each other in your lives (coworkers, roommates, neighbours etc)
Who this is NOT for
  • those in current and past abusive situations where severe harm was done
  • people you don't have a relationship with or need/want to continue being in relationship with (set a boundary if you can)

Tips for a smoooooth apology

BEFORE

Be proactive!
  • You're already doing the right thing by taking action
  • Conflict is totally normal and healthy
  • Putting things off can cause resentment or worse
Start with yourself
  • You can only control and change yourself and your feelings
  • Do some self reflection and investigate your life
Blame is bad
  • Ask "what caused this?" instead of looking for blame
  • Getting to root causes prevents future problems
Practice (or use pencil)
  • Write a draft before
  • Practice with someone you trust, or in the mirror/shower!

DURING

Remember there was a breach of trust
  • Make this the focal point for restoring the relationship
  • Practice empathy and put yourself in the other person's shoes
Don't be fake
  • Be sincere and authentic
  • If it isn't genuine, it's not an apology
  • If you don't want to apologize, don't
Use "I" statements
  • "You" statements are defensive and combative
  • Again, you can only be accountable for yourself
No "BUTS"
  • If you wanna write "i'm sorry, but..." stop right there
  • Same goes for "Sorry you felt that way"
Impact not intent
  • Practice humility, empathy, and compassion
  • Don't try to explain why you did the thing, only focus on the impact experienced by the other person

AFTER

Let go!
  • Release outcome and control
Commit to changed behaviour over being forgiven

Practice makes perfect
  • You might have to do it again, and that's okay
  • Apologizing is hard, but starting with easy ones make future ones less daunting
  • The more you do it, the better you get

Notes App-Ology Rubric 📝

Are you sick and tired of reading another bad notes app apology? So are we. Here’s a rubric we created to grade the authenticity of every apology from PR stun–ahem–offiial celebrity statements to your coworker’s long slack message.

Checklist (Total 100%)
  • Say you’re sorry (take responsibility) 35%
  • Say what you’re sorry for (name the violation) 35%
  • Describe the impact of what you did (how did it affect the other party) 20%
  • Share your plan to improve (commit to change) 10%
Extra points (add 5%)
  • Staying on topic, concise
  • Authentic voice
  • Commitment/proof of material reparations
  • Timely
  • Proactive
  • Self-reflective
Deductions (Deduct 5%)
  • Any variation of “I’m sorry but”, “I’m sorry you feel that way” “If….”, etc
  • Very fake/ scripted
  • Backtracking
  • Late/bad timing
  • Manipulative
  • Blaming
